Glossary

The shared vocabulary of interaction & UX design. Terms stay consistent across every lesson.
Affordance noun Lesson 01
A relationship between an object and an agent that determines what actions are possible. A button affords clicking; a scrollable area affords scrolling. Affordances exist whether or not they are perceived — they are about possibility, not communication. Norman, Design of Everyday Things (2013); jnd.org, "Affordances and Design"
Signifier noun Lesson 01
Any perceivable cue that communicates where an action should take place and how. The raised look of a button, an underline on a link, a "drag here" label. A signifier must be perceivable to function. Affordances determine what's possible; signifiers tell the user where and how. Norman (2013); jnd.org, "Signifiers, Not Affordances"
False affordance noun
A cue that signals an action the system cannot actually perform — e.g. text styled to look tappable that does nothing. The mirror of a hidden affordance (a real action with no signifier). Both break the user's trust. Derived from Norman; common UX usage
Discoverability noun Lesson 01
The degree to which a user can figure out what actions are possible and how to perform them. The field's real target — not total exposure. A hidden affordance is acceptable as an accelerator if a discoverable primary path also exists; it is only a defect when a critical action is reachable only by a hidden path. See affordance, signifier. Norman (2013); applied via Gaver (1991)
Mapping noun Lesson 02
The relationship between a control and its effect. The main bridge across the gulf of execution — good mapping lets a user predict what a control will do before acting. See natural mapping. Norman (2013)
Natural mapping noun Lesson 02
A mapping that exploits spatial layout or cultural convention so the relationship is obvious without a label — e.g. arranging stove knobs in the same 2×2 as the burners, or a slider that rises to mean "more." Spatial mapping is near-universal; cultural mapping is local and must be verified for your audience. Norman (2013); NN/G, "Natural Mappings"
Feedback noun Lesson 02
The system communicating that it registered an action, what it's doing, and the result. The main bridge across the gulf of evaluation. Good feedback is immediate, informative, and appropriate — its absence forces users to guess (double-clicks, duplicate orders). See response-time limits. Norman (2013); Nielsen heuristic #1
Visibility of system status phrase Lesson 02
The first usability heuristic: keep users informed about what is going on, through appropriate feedback within reasonable time. The applied form of feedback across a whole interface. Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics, #1
Response-time limits noun Lesson 02
Nielsen's three perceptual thresholds for how fast feedback must arrive: 0.1 s feels instant (show the result, no special feedback); 1 s keeps thought uninterrupted (still usually none); 10 s is the limit of held attention (owe a progress bar + estimate beyond it). Turns "good feedback" into a spec. Nielsen, "Response Time Limits"
Conceptual model noun
The simplified mental explanation a user forms of how a system works. Good design helps users build an accurate one. Covered in a later lesson. Norman (2013)
Gulf of execution noun
The gap between what a user wants to do and the actions the system makes available — "how do I even do this?" Bridged primarily by good mapping (also signifiers and constraints). Norman, User Centered System Design (1986); DOET (2013)
Gulf of evaluation noun
The gap between the system's state and the user's ability to tell whether their goal was met — "did that work?" Bridged by good feedback within the right time limits. Norman (1986); DOET (2013)
