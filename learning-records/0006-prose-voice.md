# Feedback: strip the AI-tropes from lesson prose

The learner finds the writing undercut by typical AI-prose tells. The substance is good; the packaging
reads as generated and lowers trust — which is especially damaging in a course about design judgment.

**Why it matters:** Authority comes from plain, confident statements that let the examples carry the
weight. Rhetorical flourish reads as hype and makes an expert discount the content.

**Voice rules for every lesson (and reference doc) from now on:**
- **No punchy fragment endorsements:** "that's the move," "the upgrade," "the muscle," "the senior move,"
  "the lesson landed," "the whole difference." Just say the thing.
- **Avoid the reversal cliché:** "X isn't Y — it's Z," "not X. Just Z," "isn't binary; it's…". State it
  positively.
- **No manufactured stakes:** "how users lose data and trust," "win the critique," "you'll apply forever,"
  "bounce off." Describe the concrete consequence instead, plainly.
- **Cut em-dash drama.** Prefer periods and colons. One em-dash per paragraph at most.
- **Drop intensifiers and self-praise words:** viscerally, sharp/sharper, crisp, legible-as-praise,
  genuinely, literally, exactly, precisely, the whole point.
- **Less meta-commentary** about the lesson/teaching itself ("here's the trap," "the goal of this section").
- **Trust the reader.** Don't tell them how impressive an idea is; show it and move on.
- Keep real quotes (Norman, Nielsen) and citations intact — those earn the authority.

Goal voice: a knowledgeable colleague explaining something at a whiteboard, not a landing page.
