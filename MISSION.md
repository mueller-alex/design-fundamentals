# Mission: Interaction & UX Design Fundamentals

## Why
I've practiced visual and UX design for 11 years on intuition and empathy, but I can't
articulate the *reasoning* behind my choices. I want a shared vocabulary and a set of
mental models — drawn from the field's foundational thinkers — so I can justify design
decisions, critique work rigorously, and design deliberately rather than "going with the flow."

## Success looks like
- Naming the principle at work in any interface and explaining *why* it succeeds or fails
- Defending a UI decision in a critique using precise terms (affordance, signifier, mapping, feedback…) rather than "it feels right"
- Spotting the specific principle a confusing interface violates, and proposing the fix
- Carrying a repeatable reasoning process into product work, not just an aesthetic eye

## Constraints
- Already an expert practitioner (11 yrs visual, 8 yrs UX) — skip beginner UI mechanics; go straight to the *thinking*
- Learns best from interactive wrong-way / right-way comparisons
- Wants lessons modelled on canonical sources: Norman's *The Design of Everyday Things*, interaction-design principles
- Product/UI-UX context — keep examples in digital product interfaces, not just physical doors & stoves

## Out of scope (for now)
- Visual design craft (typography, color, layout) — already strong here
- Tool tutorials (Figma mechanics)
- Research operations / usability-testing logistics — may revisit later
