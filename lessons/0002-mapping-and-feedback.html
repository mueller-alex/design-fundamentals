Mapping & Feedback

Lesson 01 was about making an element readable. This one is about making it operable: mapping lets a user predict what a control will do before acting; feedback tells them what happened after. The exercises don't describe the bugs; you operate each interface and find them.

01The two gulfs

Norman models every interaction as a loop: you form a goal, do something, then check whether it worked. Two gaps open in that loop, and they have names. — Norman, DOET (2013); seven stages of action

  • The Gulf of Execution — "how do I do this? which control?" The gap between your intention and the system's accepted actions. Mapping is the main bridge.
  • The Gulf of Evaluation — "did that work? what state am I in now?" The gap between the system's actual state and your ability to perceive it. Feedback is the main bridge.
Mapping shrinks the gulf of execution (before the action). Feedback shrinks the gulf of evaluation (after it). Most "confusing" interfaces are one specific gulf left un-bridged — and naming which one tells you the fix.

02Mapping: natural vs arbitrary

Mapping is the relationship between a control and its effect. A natural mapping exploits spatial layout or cultural convention so the relationship is obvious without a label. — NN/G, "Natural Mappings"

"When I turn on the wrong stove burner, it is not due to my lack of knowledge: it is due to poor mapping between controls and burners. Teaching me the relationship will not stop the error from recurring: redesigning the stove will."
— Don Norman, The Design of Everyday Things

04Feedback & the three time limits

Feedback is the system communicating that it registered your action, what it's doing, and the result — immediate, informative, appropriate. Its absence forces guessing, and guessing breeds errors. How fast it must arrive isn't taste; Jakob Nielsen pinned the perceptual thresholds: — NN/G, "Response Time Limits"

  • 0.1 s — feels instantaneous; show the result, no special feedback needed.
  • 1 s — noticed, but thought survives; still usually no spinner.
  • 10 s — the limit of held attention. Past this you owe a progress bar with an estimate.

10The expert's nuance

Feedback can over-communicate

More feedback isn't better. A badge on every icon, a toast for every save, a buzz for every event — and users tune all of it out, including the one that mattered. Match feedback's intensity to the event's importance: passive for routine, assertive only for consequential.

Mapping rides on convention — and convention is local

Spatial mapping (layout matches layout) is near-universal. Cultural mapping (red = stop; a switch up = on in the US, off in the UK) is not. When a mapping leans on convention, you've inherited the user's prior — verify it travels to your audience.

Optimistic feedback: answer before you know

For high-confidence actions (liking, sending), show success immediately and reconcile with the server quietly — perceived latency drops to zero. The trade: handle the rare failure gracefully, or you've replaced a slow truth with a fast lie.

13Strengthen the muscle

Turn the reasoning into a conscious procedure. Protocol → guided walk-through → real-world mission.

Part A — The protocol (run it on any action)

Find the action cycle. A goal → a do → a check. Two gulfs: execution (before), evaluation (after).
Mapping check (before). Can the user predict which control produces which effect, without a label? Natural = spatial layout or trusted convention.
Feedback check (after). Does the system confirm it heard, show what it's doing, and report the result? Immediate · informative · appropriate.
Time the feedback. <0.1 s none · ~1 s subtle/none · 1–10 s spinner · >10 s progress + estimate.
Prescribe to the right gulf. Mapping → fix layout/convention. Feedback → add the right signal at the right time. Never patch one gulf with the other's tool.
